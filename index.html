About me

I am a nutritional epidemiologist and data scientist specializing in the global burden of cardiometabolic diseases attributable to diet. My work focuses on large-scale modeling and policy-relevant research using complex mathematical modeling.

I am currently a postdoctoral scholar at the Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation (IHME) at the University of Washington. My work involves contributing to the estimation of cardiovascular and congenital, and neotanal disease burdens for the Global Burden of Disease (GBD), manuscript writing, and generating innovative research ideas. I am also leading meta-analytical work for estimating the dose-reponse effect of systolic blood pressure on heart failure.

Before joining IHME, I earned her PhD in Nutritional Epidemiology and Data Science from the Friedman School of Nutrition Science and Policy at Tufts University. My doctoral dissertation focused on analyzing intakes of sugar-sweetened beverages (SSBs) and quantifying their attributable cardiometabolic burden globally, regionally, and nationally. I quantified estimates stratified by key population subgroups including age, sex, education level, and area of residence. Furthermore, I modeled the impact of an SSB tax on SSB intakes and cardiometabolic burdens. My research aims to inform current and future policies aimed at curbing SSB consumption, focusing on social inequities to efficiently improve global population health. Notably, her doctoral work received support from the American Heart Association and has been recognized in high-impact journals and awarded by the American Society of Nutrition.

Prior to my PhD, my academic journey includes a master’s degree in Nutrition and Metabolism from the School of Medicine at Boston University and a bachelor’s degree in Nutritional Sciences from Universidad de las Américas Puebla, Mexico. Before and after her master’s, I worked as a research assistant at the National Institute of Public Health in Mexico, contributing to projects focused on improving the detection and care of cardiometabolic diseases and their complications. These experiences provided me with valuable insights into public health challenges and the importance of evidence-based research, shaping my keen interest in public health, epidemiology, and quantitative methods. In my current role as a postdoctoral scholar, I am committed to refining my quantitative and analytical skills to contribute with rigorous and meaningful research that positively impacts global health policies.
